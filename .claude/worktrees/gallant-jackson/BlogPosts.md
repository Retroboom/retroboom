# Hail of Fire — Blog Posts

---

## What the Defender Knows

Hidden unit systems are all trying to do the same thing: make unrevealed information a weapon. Pre-plotted maps and dummy blinds do this well — both players spend the game trying to leverage what hasn't been revealed yet, one side probing to learn it, the other timing reveals to maximize surprise. They just require work to set up and maintain.

I wanted to get to the same place without leaving the table to do it. What follows is my attempt at something experimental — skipping the administrative procedure to get to the tension that actually matters.

---

### A Known Force, An Unknown Distribution

When a defending unit reveals at an objective, the attacker gains something concrete: those units cannot be anywhere else. The map of where the remaining hidden force might appear just got smaller. The attacker's own uncommitted units — held off-table, waiting to see which flank opens up — can now concentrate accordingly.

Both players are doing this simultaneously. Every reveal changes the calculus. The game is a negotiation conducted through commitment, and both players are trying to be the last to show their hand while the window to act on that advantage stays open.

---

### The Pressure Is Real

This is not a system that rewards patience for its own sake. Three objectives sit on the defender's half of the table. A probe toward any one of them is a forced question: commit something to hold it, or give it up. Perpetual evasion isn't a strategy — it's conceding the game one objective at a time.

The attacker faces the opposite pressure. The defender starts with only half their force available, the rest arriving piecemeal from turn three onward — but never guaranteed. The attacker has a window of numerical advantage that closes whether they use it or not. Cautious movement doesn't spend that advantage wisely. It wastes it.

But speed has a cost. When a Hidden Team reveals immediately after an enemy movement action, the distance just moved is subtracted from the reveal threshold. A vehicle that road-moved twenty inches has effectively no minimum reveal distance remaining. An anti-tank team can appear at contact range on the other side of the nearest hedge — not lucky, but the direct consequence of moving fast through unsecured ground. The attacker who advances carefully applies pressure on their own terms. The attacker who races forward pays for it.

Neither player has a safe option. That's where the game lives.

---

### No Setup. No Maps. No Referee.

Pre-plotted maps and dummy blinds produce this tension well. What they require is time — to deploy, to record, to consult during play. Time that belongs to the game but gets spent outside of it.

HoF's hidden teams system tries to get to the same place through a reveal distance table and one subtraction rule. Nothing to record. Nothing to look up. The fog of war is present from the first move, maintained by the same advancing front line driving every other decision on the table.

It's an unconventional way to get there. But the tension it produces will feel familiar.

## Leave Your Points at the Door

Every regular group of wargamers eventually develops the same ritual. Someone proposes a game. Someone else asks what points limit. A week of list-building follows — units added, costs tallied, combinations tested against the restriction, then scrapped and rebuilt when the numbers don't fit. By the time both players sit down at the table, significant time and energy has already been spent. None of it involving dice or terrain or actual play.

---

### The "Point" of Points Systems

A points system claims that every unit's combat value can be accurately quantified and that equal points produce roughly equal games. All points systems struggle under the weight of this claim to one degree or another. Chronically over and undercosted units, meta compositions that dominate until a new edition intervenes, a persistent gap between balanced on paper and balanced on the table.

A deeper problem may be that points systems are calculating the wrong thing. The value of a Tiger tank compared to a Sherman 75 depends on terrain, engagement range, and other support. A points system picks one number and hopes it averages out.

Honestly, though, in practice the more significant issue I have with points systems is less one of accuracy, and more of convenience. Points systems just require a lot of time from players who will draft up what they want to play, cost it out, compare it to the budget, and then redesign it several more times, figuring out not only what will fit within the declared limit, but how to compose a force that best maximizes "value" within it. An optimization puzzle existing entirely outside the game.

---

### A Different Question

The Force Parity Procedure asks instead: how does this unit perform against the specific units it will face today? Both players rank every enemy unit from most to least threatening, pair them by rank, and score each pairing based on who has the advantage. The scores are compared, a handicap applied, and play begins. The whole procedure takes less than two minutes.

More importantly, it starts with a different premise entirely: bring what you want to play. A historical force. The models you've been wanting to combine. Whatever it is, that's your force. The time that would have been spent designing around a cost limit goes back to the game.

---

### The Real Comparison

No system produces perfectly balanced games. The question then, is what are you giving up in pursuit of balance, and is what you're getting worth it. Points systems have a cost — the time they demand, the inaccuracies built into their core claim, the list-building they incentivize. I wanted to see if there was another way, and the Force Parity Procedure is what I came up with. It doesn't share those costs because it isn't making the same claims. It's just cutting straight to the part we actually care about — creating a compelling match worth playing. In less than two minutes.

## Why There's No Opportunity Fire

Opportunity fire is one of those mechanics that feels essential to a WWII miniatures game. The idea is intuitive — an enemy unit moves into your line of sight, you shoot at it before it reaches cover. It recreates something real. A squad crossing an open field isn't safe just because it isn't your turn. The threat of fire is constant, not periodic. Games that handle op-fire well create genuine tension around every movement decision.

Hail of Fire doesn't have a general op-fire mechanic. Here's why.

---

### The Structural Problem

Op-fire works elegantly in games where a single stand or vehicle activates and moves to completion before the next one does. The trigger is clean: one element moves, one op-fire window opens, the move resolves, next element. The questions answer themselves because the granularity of the mechanic matches the granularity of the activation.

HoF activates units — six infantry stands, three vehicles, a platoon moving and firing together. The moment you try to apply op-fire to that structure, every question becomes unanswerable cleanly. Does it trigger when the unit declares its orders, or when each individual stand moves? If it triggers per stand, you've effectively switched to element-level activation and lost everything that makes unit activation fast. If it triggers on declaration, you're firing at an intention rather than a position. How many stands can one op-fire resolve affect? If you suppress one, can the rest move freely? In a multiplayer game, whose op-fire resolves first?

There's no version of a general op-fire mechanic that sits cleanly inside unit-level activation. Every answer to those questions either breaks the speed of play or produces a different game entirely. I tried. I couldn't find one. So instead of implementing a broken version of something familiar, I went looking for what op-fire is actually doing and whether something already in the game was doing it better.

---

### What Op-Fire Is Actually Doing

Op-fire exists to make movement dangerous. It creates uncertainty about whether you'll make it to cover before the enemy fires. It keeps defenders relevant during the attacker's turn. It punishes careless movement and rewards deliberate play.

HoF distributes that work across three mechanisms that are already present.

Random movement distances mean that when a unit declares a destination, nobody knows if they'll make it. The attacker who pushes a squad across an open field toward a hedgerow is making a genuine gamble — not because a defender might interrupt the move, but because the dice might not get them there. The uncertainty op-fire creates around movement is already built into the movement itself.

Received Fire Points accumulate without resolving. A team that takes fire during an activation carries those unresolved RFPs until they're forced to act on them. The threat of what that fire might mean — suppression, casualties — hangs over the unit without being immediately adjudicated. The defender doesn't need to interrupt the attacker's move to make fire feel dangerous. The danger is already present, deferred, waiting.

The hidden teams system means the attacker never knows what's waiting on the other side of that hedgerow. The decision to move fast or move carefully isn't just about the units they can see — it's about the ones they can't. An anti-tank team can appear at contact range as the direct consequence of a road move that covered too much ground too quickly. The cost of careless movement is priced into the reveal mechanics, not into an interruption procedure.

---

### What's Lost

Players who love op-fire will feel its absence, at least initially. There's something satisfying about the explicit moment of interruption — declaring a fire, watching the move resolve or fail, the back-and-forth rhythm of action and reaction. HoF doesn't give you that moment. What it gives you instead is a game where the same tension is present continuously rather than procedurally, where movement is dangerous not because your opponent can interrupt it but because the game is structured so that careless movement carries its own consequences.

It's a different feeling. Whether it's a satisfying substitute is something every player will decide for themselves. But it isn't an oversight. It's a deliberate trade — the explicit procedure for something quieter that's already doing the same work.
